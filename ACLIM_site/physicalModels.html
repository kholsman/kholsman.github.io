Hindcast and Projection Models

NOAA's Pacific Marine Environmental Laboratory (PMEL) produced downscaled climate forecasts from three global climate models for the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) Fourth Assessment Report (AR4) and has produced 2 downscaled climate forecasts from the IPCC AR5 report; these are available to project collaborators via the ACLIM data repository (see direct links on left). For each of these projections, boundary conditions for the ROMS-NPZ coupled model were driven by regional downscaled outputs from the following global climate models (Hermann et al. in press):

core_cfsr_combined

(1976-2012)

This is the hindcast for the Bering Sea and is a combination of the reconstructed climatology from the CLIVAR Co-ordinated Ocean-Ice Reference Experiments (CORE) Climate Model (1969-2006) the NCEP Climate Forecast System Reanalysis (CFS-R) is a set of re-forecasts carried out by NOAA’s National Center for Environmental Prediction (NCEP). Its forecasts extend nine months into the future, and it has been used to assess the potential skill of seasonal forecasts. See HERE for more info.

CCCma

(2006-2039; AR4 SRES A1B)

Developed by the Canadian Centre for Climate Modelling and Analysis, the CCCma model is also known as the CGCM3/T47 model. This model showed the greatest warming over time compared to other models tested by PMEL. See more data the AOOS:CCCma portal.

MIROC

(2006-2039; AR4 SRES A1B)

The Model for Interdisciplinary Research on Climate (MIROC)-M model developed by a consortium of agencies in Japan. Compared to other models tested by PMEL, MIROC-M was intermediate in degree of warming over the Bering Sea shelf for the first half of the 21st century. See more data the AOOS:MIROC portal.

ECHOG

(2006-2039; AR4 SRES A1B)

The ECHO-G model from the Max Planck Institute in Germany This model showed the least warming over time compared to other models tested by PMEL. See more data the AOOS:ECHO-G portal.

GFDL_RCP45

(2006-2100; AR5 RCP 4.5)

The GFDL (RCP 4.5) model: The NOAA Geophysical Fluid Dynamics Laboratory (GFDL) has lead development of the first Earth System Models (ESMs), which like physical climate models, are based on an atmospheric circulation model coupled with an oceanic circulation model, with representations of land, sea ice and iceberg dynamics; ESMs additionally incorporate interactive biogeochemistry, including the carbon cycle. The ESM2M model used in this project is an evolution of the prototype EMS2.1 model, where pressure-based vertical coordinates are used along the developmental path of GFDL’s Modular Ocean Model version 4.1 and where the land model is more adavanced (LM3) than in the previous ESM2.1.
